General Motors has announced a significant safety recall impacting its new Cadillac Vistiq SUV, halting shipments and initiating a fix for over 14,000 vehicles. The core issue involves the third-row seatbacks, which can fold down with a press of a button but, critically, do not automatically reverse if an obstruction is detected. This design flaw, similar to a recent Hyundai recall, presents a serious risk of child entrapment, prompting GM to take immediate action to protect occupants.

<Callout type="takeaway">
  The Cadillac Vistiq's third-row seat design presents a significant safety risk, prompting an immediate recall and shipment halt by GM to prevent potential child entrapment.
</Callout>

## What happened
GM's recall affects 14,540 Cadillac Vistiq models, specifically 13,629 units of the 2026 model year, manufactured between November 12, 2024, and April 9, 2026, and 911 units of the 2027 model, built from January 16, 2026, to June 15, 2026. The problem centers on the power-folding third-row seats, which can be stowed using buttons in the cargo area or on the adjacent pillar. Unlike a fully safe system, these seats will stop if they encounter an obstruction but will not automatically reverse, meaning a child or object could still be trapped.

The automaker became aware of the potential hazard after an engineer, prompted by a similar Hyundai recall in March, submitted a report through GM's "Speak Up For Safety" program. Subsequent testing confirmed the seatback's failure to return to an upright position when folded onto an obstruction. While GM acknowledges six incidents or complaints potentially related to this fault, no injuries have been reported to date. Shipments of 2027 models were halted on June 8, 2026, and 2026 production had already concluded.

## Why it matters
This recall is a critical safety measure, particularly for families who rely on three-row SUVs for transporting children. The potential for a child to be trapped and injured by a power-folding seat is a severe concern, underscoring the importance of rigorous safety testing and design. While GM's system does stop upon obstruction, the lack of an automatic reversal mechanism still leaves a dangerous window for injury, highlighting a design oversight that could have tragic consequences.

GM's proactive response, spurred by an internal safety report and a competitor's similar issue, demonstrates a commitment to addressing safety flaws, even if discovered after production. However, the incident also serves as a reminder that even new, premium vehicles can have significant safety vulnerabilities that require immediate attention and corrective action from manufacturers.

## How to think about it
For Vistiq owners, the immediate priority is to address this safety concern. While waiting for the permanent replacement parts, taking your vehicle to a dealership to have the third-row folding function temporarily disabled is a prudent step. This ensures that the risk of accidental entrapment is mitigated. It's also a good reminder for all vehicle owners to stay informed about recalls and to take them seriously, as they are issued to prevent potential harm. Always follow manufacturer instructions for safety-related repairs and modifications.

<Callout type="warning">
  Vistiq owners should immediately refrain from using the third-row power-folding seat function, especially when children or pets are in or near the vehicle, until the recall repair has been completed or the interim disablement performed.
</Callout>
