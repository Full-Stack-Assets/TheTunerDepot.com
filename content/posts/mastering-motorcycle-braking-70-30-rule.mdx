For many motorcycle enthusiasts, the art of braking is as crucial as throttle control. A fundamental guideline that often surfaces in discussions about optimal stopping power and stability is the 70/30 rule. This technique suggests a specific distribution of braking force between the front and rear wheels, aiming to maximize deceleration while maintaining control. Understanding and practicing this principle can be a game-changer for riders looking to enhance their safety and confidence on two wheels, even for those with years of experience.

<Callout type="takeaway">
The 70/30 rule suggests applying 70% front and 30% rear braking force for optimal motorcycle stability and stopping power in most situations.
</Callout>

## What happened
In most regular riding and heavy braking scenarios, the 70/30 rule advises riders to apply both the front and rear brakes, with approximately 70% of the braking force directed to the front wheel and 30% to the rear. This distribution is primarily due to weight transfer: as a motorcycle decelerates, its weight shifts forward, significantly increasing the load and traction on the front tire. Consequently, the front brake becomes the primary component for stopping the bike effectively.

Despite the front brake doing the majority of the work, the rear brake plays a vital role in maintaining stability. Some riders, often referred to as having a "squid mentality," might neglect the rear brake, believing it's ineffective due to the reduced weight on the rear wheel during braking. However, the rear brake is essential for stabilizing the bike, especially under hard braking, and is also crucial for proper corner entry and line correction. Learning to modulate both brakes simultaneously, feathering the rear while progressively loading the front, is key to achieving maximum stopping power without locking up a tire or causing instability.

## Why it matters
Proper braking technique is paramount for rider safety and overall control. Misapplying brakes, such as relying solely on the front brake or locking the rear, can lead to dangerous situations like skidding, loss of control, or even an unintentional stoppie (rear wheel lift). The 70/30 rule provides a framework that helps riders understand the dynamic interplay between weight transfer and braking force, allowing them to optimize their stopping distance while maintaining composure.

Mastering this technique builds confidence, enabling riders to react effectively in emergency situations. It transforms braking from a simple act of slowing down into a nuanced skill that enhances the bike's stability and the rider's command. Ignoring the rear brake or failing to understand the principles of weight transfer can severely limit a rider's ability to safely and efficiently bring their motorcycle to a halt.

## How to think about it
Riders should view the 70/30 rule as a foundational guideline, not an absolute law. It's a starting point for developing effective braking habits, emphasizing the importance of using both brakes in a balanced manner. The most crucial aspect is to get to know your specific bike's braking dynamics through practice. Start in a safe, open environment like a large parking lot or enroll in a rider training course. Focus on a soft initial touch, gradually increasing pressure on both the front lever and rear foot brake to feel how your bike responds. Developing this muscle memory allows your body to instinctively apply the correct pressure in a real-world scenario, maximizing your bike's braking potential while maintaining stability.

<Callout type="warning">
Be cautious applying the 70/30 rule in low-grip conditions like rain, gravel, or dirt roads, or when carrying heavy loads or passengers. These scenarios often require a more even brake distribution or greater reliance on the rear brake to prevent front wheel lockup.
</Callout>
